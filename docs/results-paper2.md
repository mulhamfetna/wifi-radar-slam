# Paper 2 results — WiFi vs LiDAR for SLAM

Paper 2 asks whether ambient-WiFi sensing can be a **drop-in LiDAR replacement** for
SLAM. This document collects the head-to-head comparison on the **same two simulated
scenes**, with the **same six metrics** (the shared `eval/metrics.py`), against the
**same footprint ground truth**. WiFi rows are the authoritative numbers from paper 1
(submitted to IEEE IoT-J, frozen at `v0.7.1`); LiDAR rows are paper 2's models A/B
(`data/lidar_geo_results.json`, `data/lidar_sionna_results.json`); the real-LiDAR
anchor is KITTI seq-04 (`data/kitti_results.json`).

Metrics (all 2-D BEV, metres except IoU): **ATE** trajectory error, **RPE** per-frame
relative error, **Chamfer** symmetric map error, **map-acc** est→GT precision,
**map-compl** GT→est coverage, **IoU** occupancy overlap. Lower is better except IoU.

## Reading the table (RQ3, and the emerging RQ1 answer)

**Localization — WiFi is competitive with, even better than, LiDAR.** Realistic
commodity-CSI WiFi with joint 2-D MUSIC localizes to **2.7 cm ATE** on the controlled
scene — better than both LiDAR models there (A 0.102 m, B 0.483 m) — and to ~9 cm on the
street, comparable to LiDAR-A's 2.6 cm. For the **trajectory/odometry** half of SLAM,
WiFi is a viable drop-in.

**Mapping — LiDAR dominates coverage; WiFi is coverage-bounded.** LiDAR-B reaches
**IoU 1.0** on the controlled wall and **5.3 m completeness** on the street; WiFi mapping
is strong only under oracle sensing on the clean wall (IoU 0.79) and collapses under
realistic CSI (IoU ≈ 0) and on the street (a single passive pass illuminates only a
subset of facades; commodity-CSI path discrimination floors map accuracy — paper 1's
thesis). For the **mapping** half, WiFi does **not** yet replace LiDAR.

**The two LiDAR models bracket reality.** A (geometric) is precise and localizes well but
low-coverage; B (diffuse physics) is dense-coverage but noisier and drifts more. A real
LiDAR sits between — so LiDAR is reported as an **A/B envelope**, not a single baseline.

**Emerging answer (RQ1):** ambient WiFi can drop-in replace LiDAR for **localization /
odometry** at a fraction of the cost (RQ5, to quantify), but **mapping** needs
enhancement — multi-pass accumulation, WiFi+LiDAR fusion (RQ4), or deep-learning
reconstruction (RQ2) — to approach LiDAR coverage. This scopes the next sub-projects.

## Fairness caveats

- **Sensor-model comparison, not a hardware benchmark.** WiFi-oracle and LiDAR-A/B are
  idealized *sensor models* on identical scenes/GT; WiFi-realistic is commodity CSI.
  LiDAR is at automotive-datasheet parameters (`OUSTER_OS1`: 120 m / ±3 cm / 360°).
- **Same GT for both**, so map-completeness penalizes *any* sensor for un-illuminated /
  occluded / sub-ring-height surfaces equally — the coverage gap is real, not a WiFi-only
  artefact (LiDAR-A shows the same on the street: completeness 17 m).
- WiFi rows are frozen paper-1 results (`docs/results-v1.md`); "—"/"bounded" mark
  metrics paper 1 did not tabulate for that scene/mode (realistic street mapping is the
  hard case paper 1 characterized qualitatively).

### Reading the cost table

The WiFi sensing package is **84–600× cheaper than the Ouster OS1-class LiDAR our A/B
models actually simulate**, and ~800–2,000× cheaper than the legacy spinning grade. On
**localization value** the gap is overwhelming: WiFi delivers **1–9 $·m** versus LiDAR's
**212–2,448 $·m** — roughly **two to three orders of magnitude better accuracy per
dollar**, because WiFi is both far cheaper *and* (on the controlled scene) more accurate.

But **WiFi cannot buy map coverage at any price**: realistic-CSI IoU ≈ 0, so `$/IoU` is
**infinite**. LiDAR is the only modality here that converts money into map coverage. That
asymmetry is the honest boundary of the "drop-in replacement" claim — and precisely the
gap **RQ2 (deep learning)** and **RQ4 (fusion)** exist to close.

### Cost caveats (honesty)

1. **LiDAR rows are priced at the OS1 tier** because that is the sensor whose parameters
   produced our measured A/B accuracy. The cheaper solid-state tiers show where the market
   is heading, but we did **not** measure their (lower) performance — pricing OS1-grade
   accuracy at $150 would be apples-to-oranges.
2. **The cost advantage is not unconditional.** Against the *cheapest* emerging automotive
   solid-state ($100–200) the headline gap narrows to **1.1–5×**, and if you must **deploy
   your own APs** (+3 APs → $130–335) WiFi can be **more expensive** (0.3–1.5×) than that
   LiDAR. The dramatic cost story depends on the **ambient-AP premise** holding.
3. **The budget 2D scanner is a price floor, not a peer** — it is not automotive-grade and
   we never compare mapping quality against it.
4. Prices carry `verified: false` where taken from press/market reporting; re-check against
   primary vendor pages before submission (see `docs/literature-paper2.md`).

## Fusion (RQ4)

Symmetric WiFi+LiDAR fusion — **not** the literature's "WiFi assists LiDAR" shape (our RQ3
result inverts that premise). **Tight**: one particle filter, `weight = w_wifi(bistatic) ×
w_lidar(scan-match)`, output map = union of WiFi reflectors + LiDAR points. **Loose**: naive
baseline (equal-weight trajectory average + map union). WiFi input is realistic commodity
CSI (joint 2-D MUSIC). `sigma_lidar = 0.5` is **fixed, never tuned per scene**.
Generated by `experiments/run_fusion.py` → `data/fusion_results.json`.

| Scene | Config | WiFi-only ATE | LiDAR-only ATE | **Fused-tight ATE** | Fused-loose ATE | LiDAR IoU | **Fused IoU** |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| controlled_wall | LiDAR A (geometric) | 0.081 | 0.102 | **0.065** | 0.056 | 0.977 | 0.913 |
| controlled_wall | LiDAR B (Sionna) | 0.081 | 0.212 | **0.044** | 0.086 | 1.000 | 0.913 |
| street_canyon | LiDAR A (geometric) | 0.281 | **0.027** | 0.218 | 0.137 | 0.149 | **0.177** |
| street_canyon | LiDAR B (Sionna) | 0.281 | 0.844 | **0.175** | 0.338 | 0.262 | **0.309** |

(WiFi-only IoU is **0.000** in every row — realistic CSI maps nothing.)

### The RQ4 answer: fusion helps **conditionally**, and the condition is *sensor parity*

In **3 of 4** configurations tight fusion **beats both solo modalities on localization** —
genuine reinforcement, not mere addition:
- controlled/A: 0.065 vs WiFi 0.081 and LiDAR 0.102
- controlled/B: **0.044** vs WiFi 0.081 and LiDAR 0.212 (a **79 %** improvement on LiDAR-B)
- street/B: **0.175** vs WiFi 0.281 and LiDAR 0.844 (a **79 %** improvement on LiDAR-B)

In the **4th** it **degrades** the better sensor: street/A, where LiDAR alone achieves
**0.027 m** and fusion drags it to **0.218 m** — an **8× regression**.

**The principle.** Fusion with *fixed equal-confidence* weighting beats both sensors when
they are **comparably accurate (or WiFi is better)**, and **harms the stronger sensor when
there is a large accuracy mismatch**. On the street, LiDAR-A is ~10× more accurate than
WiFi, so an equal-confidence WiFi likelihood pulls the filter away from LiDAR's good
solution. We report this rather than tuning `sigma` to hide it: the honest fix is
**confidence-adaptive weighting** (weight each modality by its online-estimated reliability),
which is clear future work — not a result we can claim here.

**Mapping.** Fusion's union map **adds coverage exactly where LiDAR is incomplete** (street:
IoU 0.149 → 0.177 for A, 0.262 → 0.309 for B) but **slightly pollutes an already-good map**
(controlled: 0.977/1.000 → 0.913; map-accuracy 0.25 → 0.33) because realistic-CSI reflectors
are noisy. WiFi fills gaps; it does not sharpen detail.

**Tight vs loose.** Tight coupling wins decisively where fusion helps most (controlled/B
0.044 vs 0.086; street/B 0.175 vs 0.338) — so the extra machinery earns its keep. Loose is
marginally better only in the two LiDAR-A rows, where the naive average partially shields
the strong LiDAR from the weak WiFi.

### Cost verdict: when fusion helps, it is essentially free — but it can be worse than free

The WiFi package (**$40–95**) is a **+0.2–1.2 % addition** to an Ouster-OS1-class LiDAR
(**$8,000–24,000**). So:

| Config | LiDAR-only $·m | Fused $·m | Verdict |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| controlled_wall / B | 1,696–5,087 | **354–1,060** | **~4.8× better value** — buy it |
| street_canyon / B | 6,750–20,249 | **1,408–4,217** | **~4.8× better value** — buy it |
| street_canyon / A | **216–648** | 1,753–5,253 | **8× worse value** — money for a worse result |

**Answer to "is keeping both worth it?"** When the sensors are comparable, adding WiFi to an
existing LiDAR costs **~0.5 % more** and buys a **36–79 % ATE improvement** plus modest map
coverage — an extraordinary return, and the strongest practical case in this paper for a
*hybrid* rather than a replacement. But fusion is **not unconditionally safe**: with a
mismatched pair it *destroys* accuracy you already paid for. Fusion should be deployed with
confidence-adaptive weighting, not naively.

## Enhancement (RQ2) — and a correction to paper 1

**Question:** is pure WiFi enough, or is deep-learning enhancement needed to reach
LiDAR-equivalent mapping? We closed paper-1's open loop by putting a learned path
discriminator **inside** the mapping pipeline and running a ladder:
**0** none → **1** physics heuristic (min-excess gate) → **2** RandomForest → **3** MLP.
The filter gates only which detections enter the **map**; localization is untouched.
Maps are rebuilt with the model trained on the **other** scene (leakage-free).
Scripts: `experiments/{train_map_filter,run_enhanced_map,diagnose_triangulation}.py`.

### The ladder: every rung fails

| Scene | Rung | IoU | map points | map-acc |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| controlled_wall | 0 none | 0.000 | 4 | 2.04 |
| controlled_wall | 1 heuristic | 0.000 | 4 | 2.04 |
| controlled_wall | 2 RandomForest | 0.000 | **0** | inf |
| controlled_wall | 3 MLP | 0.000 | **0** | inf |
| street_canyon | 0 none | 0.000 | 2 | 3.27 |
| street_canyon | 1 heuristic | 0.000 | 2 | 3.27 |
| street_canyon | 2 RandomForest | 0.000 | **0** | inf |
| street_canyon | 3 MLP | 0.000 | **0** | inf |

Corrected discriminator F1 (MUSIC-observable features only): held-out frames **0.00** (RF)
/ **0.00** (MLP) on the controlled wall, **0.33** / **0.45** on the street; **cross-scene
0.00–0.20**. The learned rungs reject *everything* at threshold 0.5, emptying the map.

### Why: the floor has THREE components, and discrimination is the smallest

A first diagnostic (`diagnose_triangulation.py`) showed oracle path parameters triangulate
within 1 m of a facade **100 %** of the time (median 0.25–0.32 m) while MUSIC detections
manage only 1.8 % / 23.6 %. But that comparison **conflates two causes** — MUSIC differs from
oracle both in *which paths it reports* and in *how accurately it estimates them*.

The decisive experiment (`isolate_mapping_floor.py`, `data/mapping_floor_isolation.json`)
separates them: every MUSIC detection is matched to its nearest **true** Sionna path; for
those that match a genuine single-bounce facade path, the **same path** is triangulated twice
— once with the **true** delay/AoA and once with **MUSIC's estimate**. Any degradation is
then **pure estimation error, with discrimination held perfect**.

| | controlled_wall | street_canyon |
|---|---:|---:|
| Matched a **true facade** path | 8.6 % | 2.0 % |
| Matched a **non-facade** real path (*discrimination failure*) | 2.2 % | 8.4 % |
| **No plausible match to ANY real path** (*phantom*) | **89.2 %** | **89.5 %** |

| Triangulating the *same* matched facade paths | TRUE params | MUSIC params | MUSIC range error |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| controlled_wall | **100 %** within 1 m | **2.4 %** (med 3.56 m) | **6.45 m** median |
| street_canyon | **100 %** within 1 m | **76.7 %** (med 0.68 m) | 2.11 m median |

**Three mechanisms, in order of importance:**

1. **Phantom detections (~89 % — dominant).** Nearly nine in ten MUSIC detections correspond
   to **no real propagation path at all** — they are estimator artefacts (sidelobes,
   mis-associations, noise peaks). *You cannot discriminate among real paths when most
   detections are not real paths.* This is the mechanism neither paper 1 nor our first
   analysis identified.
2. **Estimation bias.** On the controlled wall a **6.45 m median range bias** destroys
   triangulation even for correctly identified facade paths (100 % → **2.4 %** within 1 m).
   That is 3.4× the **1.87 m** path-length resolution limit at 160 MHz, so it is a **bias**, not
   a resolution bound. (The limit is c/B = 1.87 m, NOT c/2B = 0.94 m: MUSIC reports a bistatic
   PATH LENGTH, not a monostatic range.) (Paper 1 observed this range bias but did not connect it to the mapping floor.) On
   the street, by contrast, estimates of genuine facade paths are usable (76.7 % within 1 m).
3. **Discrimination failures (2–8 %).** MUSIC picks real but non-facade paths (LOS / floor /
   multi-bounce). This *is* paper 1's mechanism — it is real, but it is the **smallest** of
   the three.

### RQ2 answer

**No — a learned discriminator cannot close the WiFi mapping gap.** A filter *selects* among
detections; it can neither *invent* the real paths that 89 % of detections fail to
correspond to, nor *correct* the range bias that ruins the rest. The usable signal (2–9 % of
detections) also proved **not separable** from the phantom majority using MUSIC-observable
per-detection features — which is exactly why every rung (heuristic, RandomForest, MLP)
failed and the learned rungs emptied the map.

**Refinement/correction to paper 1.** Paper 1 concluded that realistic mapping is floored by
*path discrimination*, and that this discrimination is *learnable* (F1 ≈ 0.9) — implying a
learned filter would fix the map. That inference **does not hold**, for three reasons:
(a) discrimination among real paths addresses only the **smallest** slice (2–8 %) of the
failure, the dominant one being phantom detections (~89 %) and range bias;
(b) the reported F1 used **`elevation`**, which a single-ULA 2-D (delay–azimuth) front-end
**cannot measure** — an oracle feature, so F1 ≈ 0.9 is optimistic (our corrected F1 on
observable features: 0.00–0.45 held-out, 0.00–0.20 cross-scene);
(c) it classified **true Sionna paths**, not **MUSIC detections** — a different task.
Paper 1 is submitted and frozen; this correction is to be folded into its revision and is
reported here as a refinement, not a repudiation — its *empirical* results (the oracle map,
the 60 GHz/aperture null result) stand.

### What this does *not* show

It does **not** show that deep learning cannot help — only that **classification-based
filtering of estimated paths** cannot. The isolation experiment in fact points to where a
learned method *must* act: **suppress phantom detections and correct the range bias at the
estimator**, or bypass the estimator entirely and learn **CSI → geometry end-to-end** (the
literature precedent — a transformer reconstructing 3-D point clouds from CSI at ~1 cm ICP
RMSE, and U-Net/ViT recovering outdoor geometry from RF propagation; see
`docs/literature-paper2.md`). Notably, the street result (76.7 % of *correctly matched*
facade paths triangulate within 1 m) shows the geometry is recoverable **if** the phantom and
bias problems are solved — so the ceiling is set by the front-end, not by the physics. That
is the honest next direction, stated as future work rather than claimed as a result.

**Consequence for the paper's thesis.** WiFi's mapping gap is *not* cheaply patchable. That
makes **RQ4 fusion the practical recommendation**: adding WiFi to a LiDAR costs ~0.5 % more
and improves localization 36–79 %, while the LiDAR supplies the map coverage WiFi cannot.
