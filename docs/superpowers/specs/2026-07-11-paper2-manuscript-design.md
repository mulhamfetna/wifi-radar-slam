## Framing (approved)

**Title:** *Can Ambient WiFi Replace LiDAR for Automotive SLAM? Localization Yes, Mapping
No — and Why*

**Thesis.** Ambient WiFi is a viable **drop-in replacement for LiDAR *localization*** — it
matches or beats LiDAR at **84–600× lower sensor cost** — but **not for mapping**. We pin
down exactly why the mapping ceiling exists (it is **not** path discrimination), and show
that the practical consequence is a **hybrid**: adding WiFi to an existing LiDAR costs
**~0.5 % more** and improves localization **36–79 %**.

## Contributions (the paper's four claims)

1. **First on-vehicle / outdoor head-to-head WiFi-vs-LiDAR SLAM comparison.** Same scenes,
   same ground truth, same six metrics, with a real-LiDAR anchor (KITTI seq-04, ~0.3 % drift).
   Novelty gap confirmed by a systematic review (`docs/literature-paper2.md`): prior WiFi-SLAM
   is indoor (P2SLAM) or uses WiFi to *assist* LiDAR; none tests replacement on a vehicle.
2. **A sensor-cost analysis** — apparently the first for WiFi-vs-LiDAR SLAM — with
   **cost-normalized** accuracy ($·m) and coverage ($/IoU).
3. **A symmetric fusion study** with a cost-normalized verdict: fusion helps **conditionally**
   (the condition is *sensor parity*), and when it helps it is **essentially free**.
4. **The mapping-ceiling mechanism.** An isolation experiment shows the WiFi mapping floor is
   dominated by **phantom detections (~89 %)** and **estimator range bias (6.45 m median)** —
   **not** by path discrimination (2–8 %). This **refines paper 1's interpretation** and
   locates where a learned method must act.

## Relationship to paper 1 (handled explicitly)

Paper 1 (submitted to IoT-J, frozen at `v0.7.1`) is **cited as prior work by the same
author** and its empirical results are **reused, not re-derived** (the WiFi rows). Section
VIII states the **refinement**: paper 1's *empirical* findings stand (the oracle map, the
60 GHz/aperture null result); its **interpretation** — that path discrimination is the
mapping floor and that a learned discriminator would fix it — does not survive the isolation
experiment. Per the author's decision, the correction is to be folded into **paper 1's
revision** when IoT-J reviews arrive; paper 2 cites the corrected version. **Paper 1's frozen
submission is not edited.**

The manuscript must state this refinement plainly rather than quietly contradicting the
earlier paper — reviewers may well be the same people.

## Honesty guards (carried into the prose)

- **Simulation-based.** All WiFi/LiDAR results are ray-traced (Sionna RT); the only real-data
  element is the KITTI LiDAR anchor. Say so in the abstract, not just the limitations.
- **The cost claim is conditional.** It depends on the **ambient-AP premise**; with
  self-deployed APs WiFi can be *more* expensive than the cheapest solid-state LiDAR
  (0.3–1.5×). Report the sensitivity row.
- **LiDAR is priced at the tier we simulated** (Ouster OS1, $8–24 k) — not at a cheap
  solid-state tier whose performance we never measured.
- **Fusion is not unconditionally safe** — the street/A 8× regression is reported, not hidden.
- **Negative RQ2 result reported as a result**, with the mechanism, not buried.
- **The budget 2-D scanner is a price floor, not a peer.**
